The SIR model

We split a closed population into three compartments and follow how people move from susceptible to infectious to recovered as an epidemic unfolds.

The dynamics are governed by two rates: a transmission rate beta and a recovery rate gamma. Their ratio defines the basic reproduction number, written R0, the average number of secondary infections produced by a single case in a fully susceptible population (Anderson and May, 1991).

When R0 sits above one, the pathogen can invade and case counts grow. When it falls below one, each chain of transmission shrinks and the outbreak fades. This single threshold underpins much of how we reason about outbreaks and their control.

One number decides whether an outbreak grows or dies away. Almost everything else in the model is detail added on top of that idea. A rule of thumb for the SIR model

Why the threshold works

In code we set beta <- R0 * gamma, then integrate the system with deSolve. The mean infectious period is 1 / gamma, so a five day period gives a recovery rate of 0.2 per day. Reasoning in terms of R0 and a period is far more intuitive than a raw contact rate.

iNote

The population N is held constant, so births, deaths, and migration are ignored over the timescale of a single outbreak.
